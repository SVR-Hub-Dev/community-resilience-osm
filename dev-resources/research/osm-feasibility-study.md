# OSM for community asset mapping: a feasibility study

**The most sustainable approach is a hybrid: store public, permanent, physical features directly in OpenStreetMap, and keep operational, sensitive or programmatic data in a separate low-cost platform — with KoboToolbox, uMap and MapComplete the strongest free options.** Pure "everything in OSM" inventories are technically possible for halls, fire equipment, water tanks and bridges, but founder on OSM's verifiability rules, privacy policy, and absence of a temporal/condition data model. Pure "everything outside OSM" platforms achieve schema flexibility but expose the project to platform shutdown and stale-data risk once funding ends. The successful long-running projects (Ramani Huria in Tanzania, Keep It In The Community in England, Māori Maps in NZ) all combine an OSM commons for durable public data with a separate, governed system for the rest, anchored by a paid coordinator and a written handover plan. For Australian community groups specifically, the evidence base is thin: there is no published example of a council or community resilience network running its asset register in OSM, but the ADM+S/Australian Red Cross "Towards Resilient Communities" Ararat trial (2023–24) and Disaster Relief Australia's Big Map workshops point toward where this is heading.

## What OSM will and will not hold

OpenStreetMap has rich, mature tagging for the **physical existence of community assets**: `amenity=community_centre` (with subkeys for village halls, youth centres, cultural centres and target groups via `community_centre:for=*`), `amenity=fire_station`, `emergency=water_tank`/`fire_hydrant`/`suction_point`/`assembly_point`/`defibrillator`, `man_made=bridge` (approved as area in 2014), `social_facility=*` for staffed services, and `club=scout|social|youth|...` for organised groups that meet at a place. Operator and ownership are accommodated by `operator=*`, `owner=*`, `operator:type=community|ngo|government`. Public contact details (`phone=`, `email=`, `website=`) and council asset references (`ref:council=*`) are explicitly permitted by the Import Guidelines so long as they are real and verifiable. Lifecycle is handled by prefix tags — `disused:amenity=community_centre`, `construction:`, `proposed:`, `abandoned:` — though renderer support for these prefixes remains patchy.

The hard limits begin where data becomes **subjective, administrative, private or non-physical**. The OSM Verifiability principle requires that "independent users observing the same feature would make the same observation every time", which rules out condition ratings (`condition=fair`, `building:condition=poor` exist but were never formally proposed and are routinely contested). The Mapping Private Information policy explicitly excludes personal phone numbers, personal emails of named individuals, residents' names and home addresses; a maintenance contact `john@gmail.com` is not acceptable, while `secretary@hall.org.au` on a public-record building is. Forward-looking inspection schedules, service intervals, depreciation values and replacement costs fail the on-the-ground test and will be reverted by experienced mappers. Programs, services, classes, mobile outreach and recurring events have no home in OSM — there is no `program=*` namespace because OSM maps persistent physical features, not activities. Anything bulk-added without going through the Imports process (wiki page, plan, community consultation on `community.openstreetmap.org`, license review, conflation strategy) will also be reverted.

The **data model itself rules OSM out as an asset register**. Tags are flat strings with no validation, so `Lions Club Inc.` and `Lions Club Inc` become two separate values. Changing `owner=` overwrites history; OSM stores edit versions but has no native "as-at-2018" query, which is why OpenHistoricalMap exists as a separate project. Relations are too heavyweight to express asset hierarchies (shire → cluster → individual asset). Most fundamentally, **everything in OSM is permanent, public, ODbL-licensed open data**: a council cannot store "internal use only" notes, cannot delete contact details from history, and has no special editing authority over its own assets. The OSM Wiki's `Limitations` page is unusually direct that the data model is "very simple, in particular tags are just strings mapped to other strings".

The empirical evidence supports a narrow direct-OSM scope. **OpenFireMap and OsmHydrant.org** show the model works extremely well for objective, physical fire-water assets — German, Polish and Hungarian fire brigades use hydrant mapping as apprentice training, and the Hungarian community works directly with water utilities. Government partnerships catalogued at the OSM-for-Government wiki (TriMet in Portland, Maryland building/address imports, Utah's outdoor recreation database, Cyvl's transportation asset work) are typically **one-way contributions of objective data into OSM, not OSM as the council's system of record**. No published case study identifies a local council using OSM as its single source of truth for its asset register; the closest pattern is OSM as a contextual public layer alongside an authoritative internal database, joined by a `ref:*` identifier.

## How the OSM-adjacent platforms compare

The OSM ecosystem branches into two architectural camps. **MapComplete writes directly to OSM** through the OSM API; the website itself is static, so hosting cost is effectively zero, themes can be built in-browser via MapComplete Studio, and the data is durable in the commons. It works as a mobile PWA, requires an OSM account to edit, and is funded through 2025 by NLnet's NGI0 Entrust Fund and the Belgian government. The trade-off is that everything must conform to OSM tagging norms — there is no place to store a council asset ID, an internal contact, or a condition rating that OSM would reject. Sole maintainer Pieter Vander Vennet is a real key-person risk despite community contributors.

**uMap is the opposite architecture: separate GeoJSON storage with full schema flexibility**. The OSM-FR hosted instance carries roughly 1.45 million maps and is free; self-hosting requires Django, PostgreSQL and a small VM (~€5/month). The table editor lets non-technical users add arbitrary fields, multi-user real-time editing arrived in v3 via NLnet funding in 2024–25 (though not enabled on the public instance), and export is excellent (GeoJSON, KML, CSV, GPX, full `.umap` archive). Permissions are coarse — in open-edit mode anyone can wipe a map (issue #584) — and there is no native mobile/field workflow or data validation. **For community asset directories that need custom fields and would never be acceptable in OSM proper, uMap is the leading free option.**

**HOT's Field Mapping Tasking Manager (Field-TM)** is the right answer for organised, larger-scale field surveys. It combines a task-allocation web app with ODK Central and a custom ODK Collect fork, supports XLSForms (the humanitarian-sector standard for structured questionnaires with validation, conditional logic and offline collection), and handles role-based permissions for project managers, field managers, mappers and validators. It has been deployed in Nepal (3,400 buildings in 15 days in Janakpur), Liberia, Cambodia, Sierra Leone and elsewhere. The 2025 rename from FMTM to Field-TM reflects a deliberate pivot to allow non-OSM use cases. It is overkill for small volunteer projects but the strongest fit when an NGO is mapping ≥10 mappers across hundreds of features. Tasking Manager itself, by contrast, is a _coordination_ layer for armchair OSM mapping campaigns, not for community asset registers.

Outside the OSM-native stack, **KoboToolbox is the standout external platform** for community asset mapping in 2026. The free Community Plan provides 5,000 submissions/month and 1 GB storage to nonprofits, public-good and research organisations with budgets under USD 250k/year. Critically for this question, KoboToolbox supports **OpenStreetMap as a default basemap** alongside OpenTopoMap, Humanitarian OSM and ESRI imagery, with custom GeoJSON/KML overlays and offline MBTiles. Geopoint, geotrace and geoshape question types cover points, lines and polygons; the web form-builder is widely cited as the easiest in the humanitarian sector; KoboCollect runs fully offline on Android. Institutional sustainability is strong — Kobo Inc. is backed by UNHCR, World Bank, USAID and OCHA via the Joint Data Center initiative, with 32,000+ organisations using it including all UN agencies. **For a non-technical Australian community group needing custom fields, photos, GPS and offline collection on a free tier, Kobo is the most defensible choice.** The underlying ODK Central engine is available for groups wanting more direct control.

The remaining options serve narrower niches. Ushahidi is still alive (v2025.04 release) but commit cadence has slowed and its strengths in SMS/social-media ingestion are mismatched to structured asset inventory; Crowdmap's January 2021 shutdown is a cautionary tale. **QField + Mergin Maps** is the strongest open-source field stack when a QGIS-literate person is available, with full data ownership, GeoPackage native storage, excellent offline support, and successful deployments with non-technical health workers via the National Library of Medicine's Community Health Maps program. EpiCollect5 is the friendliest free tool but its Oxford team explicitly recommends Kobo/ODK for mission-critical work. Felt is delightful for collaborative web maps but has moved survey/data-collection features into its Enterprise tier. Survey123 with ArcGIS Field Maps dominates Australian council asset programs (City of Mitcham, Parramatta, North Sydney, Brisbane CC) but has no genuine free tier and is structurally inappropriate for community groups.

## What sustainability looks like in practice

Across ten case studies, **the determinant of long-term success is institutional, not technical**. **Ramani Huria** in Dar es Salaam has run for a decade because it combined three funding tranches (1.0, 2.0, 2.5), an institutional anchor (universities, the Ministry of Lands, the Multi-Agency Emergency Response Team), the deliberate decision to house data in OSM where it survives funder withdrawal, and the spin-off of OpenMap Development Tanzania as a local entity carrying organisational memory. **Resilient Hills & Coasts** in South Australia survived an SA Sector Agreement expiring in 2020 only because a new agreement was signed in 2021–25 reinstating the paid coordinator role; the project lost momentum in the gap, demonstrating the bus-factor risk that civic-tech vendor Civic.ly captures bluntly: _"95% of councils still rely on spreadsheets, paper, and one person's memory. When that person leaves, years of knowledge walk out the door."_

The failure modes are consistent and predictable. **Christchurch Recovery Map**, the textbook Ushahidi deployment after the February 2011 earthquake, worked beautifully during activation but went dormant within months — the Cawley/Hester analysis flagged "long-term sustainability of social capital" as the explicit failure mode. Crowdmap, SwiftRiver, Ping/Rollcall and Crisis Net all subsequently shut down. Martin Dittus's UCL PhD on Missing Maps found that **attendees at one-off corporate mapathons rarely keep mapping afterwards**, undermining the volunteer-recruitment-via-events model. License incompatibility is another quiet killer: ODbL share-alike means OSM data cannot be merged into a restricted-use council asset database without contaminating one side or the other, which is why English parishes default to Parish Online (~£30–£100/year) over OSM despite OS PSMA giving them free access to authoritative data.

Two handover patterns deserve highlighting. **Keep It In The Community**, mySociety's register of England's Assets of Community Value, was funded by a one-off Power To Change grant; when mySociety's interest waned, the project was transferred intact to the Plunkett Foundation rather than abandoned — the model for funder-built-in succession planning. **Māori Maps** migrated _from_ Google Maps _to_ OSM/Mapbox via Christchurch firm Orbica in 2021, citing data sovereignty: "Bringing the data and mapping of ancestral marae back to Aotearoa is now a reality." Apple Maps' 2025 iwi partnership (Ngāti Whātua Ōrākei, Ngāti Kuia, Tūhourangi) followed the same logic — share what is already public, keep sensitive sites under iwi control. This is why Indigenous Mapping Workshop Australia teaches Google Earth, QGIS, Mapbox and Fulcrum, but **not** OSM: OSM's permanent public history is structurally incompatible with cultural protocols around sacred sites.

Three onboarding insights for non-technical users. First, MapComplete's thematic restriction (only one topic at a time) is explicitly designed as "easy mode" for OSM, but still requires an OSM account, which already filters out a portion of community members. Second, KoboToolbox's no-login web forms remove that barrier entirely and are the lowest-friction option for genuine non-technical users. Third, the South East Landcare experience in NSW (five Zoom training sessions for District Landcare networks, 13–16 attendees each, run with NSW Local Land Services GIS staff) found that _"the greatest learning was that these mapping skills had always been within our community"_ — implying training and a community of practice are at least as valuable as tool selection. Ramani Huria budgets several days for non-engineers learning structural attribute collection.

## The Australian picture is sparse but instructive

There is no Australian community asset mapping project of Ramani Huria's scale or duration, and OSM AU is small relative to the OSM-UK or OSM-DE communities. Statutory hazard mapping (NSW RFS Bush Fire Prone Land, NSW SES, CFA) is run by state agencies under the EP&A Act and RFS Act and is structurally not community-editable. The dominant **Australian community asset/risk mapping pattern is Disaster Relief Australia's "Big Map"** workshops, which have been delivered in Willawarrin, Mogo (post-Black Summer), Collie WA, Richmond Catchment, Gawler River, Bega, Snowy Mountains, Forbes, Macleay Catchment and Central Coast, with Belgrave and Kiah scheduled for 2025. Big Map is a wargame-derived face-to-face workshop using a giant printed floor map; the artefact is the engagement, the output is a Project Resilience Report feeding Local Emergency Management Plans, and crucially **it is not an OSM project and not a maintained dataset** — which avoids the stale-data problem at the cost of permanence.

The leading research-grade Australian effort is the **ADM+S/Humanitech "Towards Resilient Communities" platform** (Swinburne ARC Centre of Excellence + Australian Red Cross, February 2024), trialled in Ararat, Vic in late 2023 with the Grampians bushfire-affected community. The published summary emphasises generating data "with and within communities" but does not name the underlying stack — a real evidence gap. Esri/ArcGIS Field Maps + Survey123 dominate paid council asset programs; RapidMap (Brisbane CC sports fields, Sutherland Shire parks, Maribyrnong stormwater, City of Casey parks) is the local commercial competitor; Adelaide Plains Council uses GIS Cloud's MDC for road sign inventories as a "budget-friendly" small-council exemplar; Fulcrum is used by some Australian local-government natural-resources programs but rising prices have prompted alternatives reviews. Aboriginal community asset mapping is governed by First Nations data sovereignty and the OSM AU community has explicit guidelines requiring consultation before mapping First Nations boundaries, with `boundary=aboriginal_lands` not adopted in Australia.

Funding for the Australian context flows through the **National Emergency Management Agency Disaster Ready Fund** (replaced Preparing Australian Communities), Queensland's Resilience and Risk Reduction Fund (~$14.7M for 25 projects in 2023–24), the SA Disaster Resilience Grant Program, and recognition through the Resilient Australia Awards administered by the Australian Disaster Resilience Knowledge Hub.

## A practical recommendation framework

The decision logic that emerges from this evidence is unusually clean. Use OSM directly when the data is **public, permanent and physical** — community halls, defibrillators, evacuation centres, fire hydrants, water tanks, bridges, public toilets, drinking fountains, trail heads, cycle parking. Use **MapComplete for the editing layer** if non-technical volunteers will contribute, building a custom theme via Studio. Use **uMap for thematic dashboards or community directories** that need fields OSM would reject (council asset IDs, accessibility audit results, local notes). Use **KoboToolbox + ODK Collect** for any project requiring offline mobile capture with structured forms — accessibility audits, community building condition surveys, water tank inspections — particularly when nonprofit free-tier limits suffice. Use **Field-TM** when scale and coordination demand it. **Never put sensitive data in OSM**: women's refuges, sacred sites, individual contact details, vulnerable household locations — OSM history is permanent and dump-replicated worldwide, deletion does not erase. For council asset registers integrated with maintenance workflows and statutory records, accept that **proprietary tools (FixMyStreet Pro, Parish Online, ArcGIS Field Maps) remain structurally better** than the OSM ecosystem, and that this is unlikely to change.

The architectural pattern most likely to last a decade combines four layers: (1) **OSM as the canonical store for public, physical, verifiable attributes**, joined to council/community systems by a stable `ref:*` identifier; (2) **a Kobo or uMap workspace** for operational, sensitive or non-OSM-compliant attributes against the same identifier; (3) **MapComplete or Every Door** as the field-editing layer for OSM contributions; (4) **a written governance plan** specifying paid coordinator funding across at least three cycles, a documented onboarding pipeline trained to a bus factor of 3+, an annual review cadence with stale-data flagging, and a successor organisation pre-identified in the model of mySociety's handover to Plunkett Foundation.

The companion architecture document, [community-asset-mapper-architecture.md](community-asset-mapper-architecture.md), realises layer (2) for the Australian context as a **Neon Postgres + PostGIS** database (free tier) holding the operational schema, **Cloudflare R2** for photo blobs, and a **SvelteKit app on Cloudflare Workers** that joins to OSM via `ref:community_asset` — chosen for zero hosting cost, no proprietary vendor lock-in, and standard open-source primitives that a successor maintainer can pick up without specialist knowledge.

The two pitfalls to design around at the outset are **license contamination** (ODbL share-alike vs. restricted council licenses force a clean separation) and **the stale-data trap** (Ushahidi-style activation maps that have no maintenance plan should be designed as event artefacts with publication endpoints, not as living registers).

## What this changes about how to think about the question

The question "can OSM hold a community asset register" turns out to be a category error — OSM is a global commons of physical geography, not an asset management system, and it is best understood as one tier of a stack rather than as the system. The genuine question is what _belongs_ in the commons versus what belongs in a community-controlled store, and the answer has shifted in the last three years as MapComplete, uMap v3, KoboToolbox's free Community Plan, Field-TM, and ODK Central's 2025 entity-mapping features matured into a coherent open toolchain. **A small Australian community organisation today can stand up a defensible, low-cost asset mapping capability for under AUD 100/year**, choosing between writing public attributes to OSM via MapComplete and operational attributes into a free Kobo workspace, with no proprietary vendor dependency. What it cannot easily buy is the institutional anchor and paid coordinator role that every long-running case study identifies as the actual binding constraint. The technology is no longer the limiting factor; **organisational continuity is**, and that is where funders and community resilience networks should focus their attention.
